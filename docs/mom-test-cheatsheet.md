# The Mom Test — Cheat-Sheet

> "Customer conversations are bad by default. It's your job to fix them."

The premise: if you ask bad questions, even your **mom** will lie to you (out of love). Good questions are ones nobody can lie about, regardless of how much they like you. The data problem is always your fault, never theirs.

---

## The Three Rules (memorise these)

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** The idea is yours to evaluate — don't outsource that judgment to them.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.** People are unreliable narrators of their own future behaviour.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** Aim for ~1/3 talking at most. Pre-write your questions so you can shut up during the call.

---

## The Three Types of Bad Data (recognise & neutralise)

| Bad data | What it sounds like | What it actually means | What to do |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Compliments** | "Cool!", "I love it.", "Great idea." | Almost nothing. Politeness signal. | Deflect, ignore the warm fuzzies, return to their life. |
| **Fluff** (generics, hypotheticals, future) | "I usually…", "I would…", "I might…" | They're guessing or performing. | Anchor to a specific past instance. "When did that last happen?" |
| **Ideas** (their feature requests) | "You should add X.", "What you really need is Y." | Symptom of an underlying problem. Don't take the order — diagnose it. | Dig: *why*, *what would it do for you*, *what else have you tried*, *why didn't that work*. |

**Compliments and ideas are sirens.** They feel like progress; they aren't. Write down the *problem* behind the idea, not the idea.

---

## Good Questions vs. Bad Questions

❌ **Bad** (invites lies, opinions, hypothetical commitments)
- "Do you think it's a good idea?"
- "Would you buy a product that did X?"
- "How much would you pay for Y?"
- "Would you use a tool that…?"

✅ **Good** (about real past behaviour, real money, real time, real workarounds)
- "Talk me through the last time you did X."
- "What are you using to solve this today?"
- "What did you do the last time it broke?"
- "Walk me through your workflow yesterday."
- "How much time / money does this currently cost you?"
- "What have you already tried? Why didn't it stick?"

**Rule of thumb:** If the answer requires them to predict their own future behaviour or imagine a hypothetical, the question is bad.

---

## The Five Deadly Questions (Fitzpatrick's go-to set)

Use these to diagnose any problem area:

1. **"What's the hardest part about [doing the thing]?"**
2. **"Tell me about the last time that happened."**
3. **"Why was that hard?"**
4. **"What, if anything, have you done to solve it?"**
5. **"What don't you love about the solutions you've already tried?"**

Q5 is the gold mine — it's where they tell you what *your* product needs to be without you pitching it.

---

## Deflecting Compliments, Anchoring Fluff, Digging Beneath Ideas

- **Compliment →** "Thanks, but ignore that for a second — *do you actually have this problem?* When did you last hit it?"
- **Generic ("I usually…") →** "When was the last time? Walk me through it."
- **Hypothetical ("I would…") →** "Have you ever actually done that? What stopped you / what happened?"
- **Future ("I'm planning to…") →** "What's the trigger? Is anything blocking you from doing it this week?"
- **Idea / feature request →** "Interesting — *what would that let you do that you can't do today?* What have you tried instead?"

## Pain Is Currency: Distinguishing Must-Haves From Nice-to-Haves

Ask yourself after each interview:

- Are they **actively trying to solve this problem right now**? (Bought tools, hired people, built workarounds.)
- Have they **spent money or significant time** on it? (Strongest signal.)
- Is there an **emotional charge** when they describe it? (Frustration, resignation, eye-rolling.)
- Or are they just **mildly annoyed in theory**?

Mild-annoyance problems don't pay rent. Look for **hair-on-fire** problems — they're rare and worth confirming hard.

---

## Commitment & Advancement (the only real signals)

Every meeting must produce one of: **a clear next step**, **a clear "no"**, or **new information that changes your plan**. "Great chat, let's stay in touch" = a polite "no". Treat it as one.

**The three currencies people pay with (in order of weakness → strength):**

| Currency | Cheap (almost meaningless) | Medium | Expensive (real signal) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time** | Vague follow-up | A scheduled, calendar-blocked next meeting | Multi-hour workshop, trial, pilot |
| **Reputation** | Verbal compliment | Intro to a colleague | Public endorsement, intro to their boss or budget-holder |
| **Money** | "I'd pay for that" | LOI, pre-order, deposit | Signed contract, prepayment |

**Always ask for a commitment that moves the relationship one step forward.** If they won't pay any of the three currencies, you don't have a customer — you have a fan.

## The Three Conversation Modes

Know which one you're in *before* the meeting, and don't mix them.

1. **Scoping (early).** Goal: understand a customer segment's life, problems, workflows, constraints. No pitching. Wide-open questions.
2. **Signal-hunting (middle).** Goal: pressure-test specific risky assumptions. Targeted, narrower questions. Still mostly listening.
3. **Selling / advancing (late).** Goal: secure a commitment (pilot, deposit, intro). Now you're allowed to talk about the idea.

Mixing #1 and #3 in the same call is the #1 source of garbage interview data.

## Pre-Meeting Prep (5-minute checklist)

Before *every* call:

- [ ] **Write down your 3 biggest current questions / riskiest assumptions.** If you can't, you're not ready to interview.
- [ ] **What would surprise you?** Predict the answers. If the real answers match, you learned little — refine the questions.
- [ ] **Identify the cheapest test that would falsify the riskiest assumption.** Is an interview even the right tool, or do you need a behavioural test?
- [ ] **Pick the conversation mode** (scoping / signal / advancing) and *stick to it*.
- [ ] **Define the commitment you'll ask for** at the end (intro, follow-up demo, paid pilot, etc.).
- [ ] **Pre-write 5–8 open questions** rooted in past behaviour. Order easy → harder.
- [ ] **Decide what counts as a green / yellow / red signal** so you don't rationalise afterwards.

## During the Meeting

- **Frame casually, not formally.** "I'm trying to understand how [role] actually [does X] day-to-day — would love to learn from you, this isn't a sales pitch." Lowers their defences and removes the temptation to flatter.
- **Talk ~30% of the time.** Use silence as a tool — most useful answers come after 3 seconds of awkward quiet.
- **Anchor every answer to a real, recent, specific event.** "Tell me about the last time" beats every other question.
- **Follow emotion.** Frustration, laughter, sighs, swearing — these are sign-posts to real problems. Dig there.
- **Take verbatim quotes.** Not paraphrases. The exact words become your marketing copy and your evidence.
- **Note signals separately from facts.** Mark `[FACT]`, `[QUOTE]`, `[SIGNAL]`, `[NEXT-STEP]`, `[IDEA-FROM-THEM]` in your notes.
- **Push for the commitment before you hang up.** "Based on what you said, the natural next step is X — does that make sense for you?"
- **Never end without a clear next step or an explicit no.**

## Red Flags Mid-Call (correct in real time)

- You're talking more than they are.
- You're pitching when you're supposed to be scoping.
- They're stringing together compliments.
- Answers are all in the conditional / future tense.
- You're hearing what you wanted to hear.
- They're listing features instead of describing pain.
- You can't articulate, in one sentence, the *specific past event* they just described.
- There's no clear ask on the table for the close.

When you spot one: **break the pattern**. "Sorry, can I rewind — when did this actually last happen to you?"

---

## Finding the Right People to Talk To (Customer Slicing)

A vague "small business owners" segment is useless. Slice down until the segment is so narrow you know exactly where to find them and what they care about.

**Iteratively ask:**
1. **Who within this group wants it most?**
2. **Within that subset, who would benefit most / has the most acute pain?**
3. **Within that subset, who has the budget / authority / urgency to act?**
4. **Where do those people already gather?** (Conferences, Slack groups, subreddits, LinkedIn segments, professional bodies.)

Stop slicing when the answer to "where do I find 10 of them this week?" becomes obvious.

---

## Cold / Trade-Show / Hallway Conversations

- Lead with **their** world, not yours. "I'm researching how [role] handles [domain] — what's the most frustrating part of that for you?"
- 5 minutes is enough. The goal is **qualification + scheduling a real call**, not getting answers on the spot.
- Always exit with a contact handle and a stated next step.

---

## Post-Meeting Debrief (do this within 30 min, before memory decays)

- [ ] **Top 3 verbatim quotes.**
- [ ] **Did I get answers to my pre-written risky questions? Y/N each.**
- [ ] **What surprised me?** (This is the real learning.)
- [ ] **What hypothesis did this confirm? Which did it weaken / kill?**
- [ ] **Pain score (1–5): how strong is the underlying problem?** Justify with evidence, not vibe.
- [ ] **Commitment obtained?** What currency, what magnitude?
- [ ] **Next concrete step + date.**
- [ ] **If I were running this call again, what would I cut and what would I dig harder on?**
- [ ] **Update the segment hypothesis** if anything shifted.

## The Big Three Failure Modes (Fitzpatrick's "why meetings go bad")

1. **No clear framing / question.** You're "just chatting." You'll get fluff.
2. **No push for commitment.** You leave with warm feelings and zero advancement.
3. **Fear of asking the scary question.** The one that might kill the idea. Ask it *first*, not last — getting a "no" early is a gift.

---

## Mental Models to Carry Into Every Interview

- **The data is your fault, not theirs.** Bad data = you asked badly.
- **Compliments are warning signs, not progress.** Track them; don't celebrate them.
- **Anything they say about the future is fiction.** Anchor to the past.
- **The goal of early conversations is to disqualify your idea**, not validate it. You're hunting for the answer that kills the plan cheaply.
- **You learn nothing while talking.** Shut up.
- **No commitment = no progress**, no matter how nice the conversation felt.

---

## One-Sentence Summary

**Talk about their life, not your idea; ask about specific things they actually did, not generic things they might do; and never leave a meeting without a real commitment of time, reputation, or money.**
